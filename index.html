Beyond Fixed-Goal Delivery

Online POMDP Planning for Target Interception in Crowds

Himanshu Gupta, Kelvin Aladum, Nisar Ahmed, Bradley Hayes, Zachary Sunberg

University of Colorado Boulder

Paper Video BibTeX Code (coming soon)

A robot must intercept a moving target while navigating safely among humans whose intentions are unobservable.

Abstract

Planning to meet a moving objective in a crowd

Target interception in crowded environments requires reaching a moving objective while navigating among multiple uncertain human agents. Since human navigation intent is not directly observable, the robot must reason over multiple possible future interaction outcomes. We formulate interception in crowds as a partially observable Markov decision process and solve it online using tree search under a fixed computational budget. In this setting, the action-space structure directly shapes the search tree and how computational effort is allocated. We compare a sequential path–speed planner, which first plans a spatial path and then modulates speed along it, and a unified planner that jointly branches over steering and speed within tree search. Across simulations with up to 200 humans, both approaches perform similarly at low crowd density but diverge sharply as density increases. In dense settings, the sequential planner drops by over 30% in safe interception rate and requires around 40% more time to intercept than the unified steering–speed planner, revealing a structural limitation of spatial restriction.

Video overview

Core finding

Dense crowds expose the cost of spatial restriction

Interception couples where the robot should meet the target with when it can safely arrive. As crowd density increases, planning speed along a fixed path becomes increasingly restrictive.

P-LSP

Predict a target position, then plan a path

Predictive LSP uses Hybrid A* to plan a kinodynamically feasible path to the target's predicted future position. Tree search then controls only speed along that fixed path, restricting how the robot can respond when crowd interactions change where and when interception is feasible.

ESP

Steering and speed together

Extended Space Planning jointly branches over steering and speed, allowing tree search to revise the spatial trajectory when interactions change the feasible interception time and location.

Dense crowds

31-point safe-success gap

At 200 humans, ESP retains 97% safe success, compared with 66% for P-LSP, even though both approaches perform similarly at low crowd density.

Results

Spatial flexibility preserves safe interception as crowds grow

We evaluate each planner over 500 paired trials at every crowd size, from 50 to 200 humans. The planners use the same observations, belief representation, reward, and 0.5-second planning budget.

97% ESP safe success at 200 humans
66% P-LSP safe success at 200 humans
31 pts ESP advantage in safe success over P-LSP
98 vs. 114 s ESP versus P-LSP interception time with target estimation

The advantage persists when the target is only intermittently observed

In the secondary study, a particle filter estimates target state from intermittent detections. At 200 humans, ESP maintains about 90% success, compared with 83% for P-LSP. ESP intercepts in about 98 seconds on average, versus 114 seconds for P-LSP.

ESP keeps moving instead of hesitating

Compared with P-LSP, ESP exhibits less idle time, higher average speed, and fewer sudden-braking events. It can travel farther while still intercepting sooner, indicating active maneuvering around people rather than stalling along a restricted path.
